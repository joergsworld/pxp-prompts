## How to Use These Prompts

A prompt is not a command. It is a conversation starter. The better you describe what you actually need, the better the response.

### Four Things That Matter

**1. Say what you actually want.** Before you type anything, answer this for yourself: what would a great response look like? Not "help me with my strategy" but "help me find the three weakest assumptions in my go-to-market plan, and for each one, tell me what evidence would change your mind." The gap between a vague request and a specific one is where most quality is lost.

**2. Give context generously.** AI does not know your situation unless you describe it. Think of it like briefing a smart colleague who just walked into the room. They are capable but they have no context. Your role, your industry, what you have already tried, why this matters now — include it.

**3. Be specific about the shape of the answer.** "Write me a strategy" produces something generic. "Give me three strategic options, each in two sentences, with the key risk for each" produces something useful. Specificity is not rigidity — it is giving the AI enough structure to be genuinely helpful.

**4. Build the conversation, do not restart it.** The first response is rarely the best one. Push back. Ask why. Say "that is too generic" or "go deeper on point two" or "now argue the opposite." A five-turn conversation will produce something far better than five separate prompts.

### Why These Prompts Are Written in Plain Text

A prompt you cannot read is a prompt you cannot trust. If you do not understand what it tells the AI to do, you cannot judge whether the output is good. The prompts here are designed to be understood first, used second. Read them. Understand the logic. Then paste them and make them yours.

### Which AI to Use

These prompts work with Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or whichever AI you prefer. The principles are the same across systems. Different systems have different strengths — experiment.

## The Meta-Principle

The best prompts come from clear thinking. If you know what you want, describing it to an AI is easy. If you do not know what you want, the act of trying to describe it often clarifies your thinking. Either way, you win.

This is why prompting is a thinking skill, not a technical skill. And why the prompts in this series are designed to make you think, not just to produce output.
