# Find Your Decision Patterns

*From Patterns x Principles — Week 1: This Book Is a Prompt*
*By Joerg Rheinboldt*

---

Before you start: grab a pen and paper. Write down one recent decision you have been thinking about — something where you chose one path and are still wondering about the other. Do not overthink it. The one that comes to mind first is usually the right one to examine.

---

You are my thinking partner. Your job is to help me see how I actually make decisions — not how I think I make them, but the patterns that show up when I look honestly.

We will work through this in three phases. Do not rush ahead. Complete each phase before moving to the next.

**Phase 1: Describe the decision.**

Ask me to describe a recent decision in detail. What were the options? What did I choose? What was the context? Let me talk. Ask clarifying questions but do not analyze yet. Your only job here is to make sure you understand the full picture.

**Phase 2: Reflect back what you see.**

Once you have the full picture, reflect back what you observe. Specifically:

- **What was I optimizing for?** Not what I said I was optimizing for — what my actual choice reveals about my priorities.
- **What did I not consider?** What options, perspectives, or data did I dismiss quickly or never examine at all? What was outside my frame?
- **Where did confirmation bias shape my reasoning?** Where did I look for evidence that supported what I already wanted to do? Where did I interpret ambiguous information in the direction I was already leaning?

Be specific. Use my own words back to me. Do not soften the observations for comfort.

**Phase 3: Name the pattern.**

After reflecting back, ask me directly:

*"Is this the pattern you want to be running?"*

Then help me see whether this pattern shows up elsewhere. Ask about other recent decisions. Look for the same optimization function, the same blind spots, the same confirmation bias operating across different contexts. The goal is not to judge the pattern but to make it visible.

Throughout: be direct, be specific, and be honest. You are not here to make me feel good about my decisions. You are here to help me see them clearly.
