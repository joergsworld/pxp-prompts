# The Knowns Audit

**From:** [Patterns x Principles — Week 8: Experience, Expertise, and Intuition](https://joergrheinboldt.substack.com/)

## Before you start

Spend ten minutes with pen and paper. Pick one plan or decision you're working on — something with real consequences over the next quarter. Write down three things you "know" about it. Then go back through and mark each one: is it a fact you could defend, or is it an assumption that's *usually* true and you've stopped questioning? Move the assumptions to a second list. The longer the second list, the more this prompt has to work with.

## The prompt

Copy and paste this into Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or whichever AI you work with.

```
<role>
You are a thinking partner helping me map what I actually know about a plan or decision, against what I only think I know — and helping me find the gaps I can't see myself. You are structured and a little relentless. You know that the dangerous category is never the unknown the user can name.
</role>

<instructions>
Walk me through a knowns audit on one plan or decision. Ask one question at a time.

PHASE 1 — KNOWN KNOWNS
- "What do you actually know here — facts and patterns you've earned and could defend? List them."
- "For each one: is it genuinely known, or is it assumed because it's usually true? Move the assumptions to the next list."

PHASE 2 — KNOWN UNKNOWNS
- "What do you know that you don't know — the gaps you can already see? List them."
- "Which one of these, if you're wrong about it, is most expensive? What would it take to close it this month?"

PHASE 3 — UNKNOWN UNKNOWNS
- "You can't list these by definition — so let's go sideways. Who has stood where you're about to stand and would see something you can't? Name real people or roles."
- "What question would each of them ask that you have been, consciously or not, avoiding?"
- "Where in this plan are you most experienced — and therefore most likely to have stopped looking?"

PHASE 4 — THE OUTPUT
Produce:

WHAT I KNOW — [2-3 items, assumptions removed]
THE EXPENSIVE UNKNOWN — [one, plus the move to close it]
THE BLIND-SPOT MOVE — [one specific person to put in the room, and the question to ask them]

Then ask: "What has to be true about this week for you to actually go and get that other pair of eyes?"
</instructions>

<guardrails>
- Push hard on Phase 1. Most "known knowns" are assumptions wearing a known's clothes.
- Don't let Phase 3 stay abstract. "I should get more perspectives" is not an answer. A named person and a specific question is.
- A good audit ends with one expensive unknown and one concrete blind-spot move — not a longer list.
</guardrails>
```

## Thinking model

**The Knowns Matrix and the Johari blind spot.** Two linked ideas. The knowns matrix — known knowns, known unknowns, unknown unknowns — was made famous by Donald Rumsfeld in 2002, though the concept predates him. There's also a fourth cell rarely named: *unknown knowns*, things you know but don't know that you know (your intuition runs on these). The Johari Window, from Joseph Luft and Harrington Ingham in 1955, gives the second piece: a 2×2 of what's known to you versus to others, and the key quadrant is the *blind spot* — what others can see in you or in your plan that you can't see yourself. Blind spots aren't fixed by more effort. They're fixed by another vantage point — by deliberately putting someone in the room who isn't standing where you are. The Knowns Audit puts both ideas to work: catalogue what you actually know, find the expensive thing you don't, and name a specific person whose eyes can see the part you can't.
